# Relationship v4 — Ground-Truth Per-Concept Tables

> [!note] Status
> **Methodology.** Per-concept relationship tables synthesized by the user from the wiki's concept pages and literature analysis, reconciling conflicting classifications across the identified source papers.
>
> **Scope.** Includes concepts appearing in ≥3 identified sources, plus closely-related sibling concepts added to disambiguate (e.g., self-awareness vs. situational-awareness, evaluation-faking vs. evaluation-awareness).
>
> **Caveats.**
> - *User-curated, not exhaustive* — tables reflect user judgment over the identified source set; not every relation in every paper is captured.
> - *Verbatim ≠ definition* — the Verbatim column shows the textual evidence for an edge; the canonical label is the user's interpretation of how that evidence maps onto the five families.
> - *Contested hierarchies preserved* — some edges have disputed direction (PS↔SP, Scheming↔AF, EA↔SA). Both directions are recorded under the respective source concepts; see warning boxes below.
> - *Living document* — tables are revised as new sources are ingested.

## How to read

- One table per source concept. Each row is one outgoing edge.
- **Verbatim** = exact phrase from the source paper (e.g., "first empirical example of", "is a sub-class of").
- **Canonical** = kebab-case relation in one of five families.
- **Notes** = caveats and links to debate pages.


> NOTE: `manifests-through` means "manifests through" → among other behaviors, vs. "can serve as tactic for".
>
> Inverse pairs in use: `requires` ↔ `required-for`; `subsumes` ↔ `form-of` ; `manifests-through` ↔ `form-of` ; `component-of` ↔ `consists-of`; `motivates` ↔ `motivated-by`. `form-of` is bivalent: it inverts `subsumes` when B is a *kind* of A, and `manifests-through` when B is an *observable expression* of A.


>[!Relationship types]
>* **"Subsumes"** is a relationship of _categorical inclusion_. If A subsumes B, then B is a type or subset of A — B falls under the broader category A. Saying "self-preservation subsumes shutdown resistance" means shutdown resistance is _one kind of_ self-preservation behavior, sitting inside a larger category that also includes things like resisting modification, acquiring resources to ensure continuity, avoiding being copied over, etc. The focus is taxonomic: where does shutdown resistance fit in the conceptual map?
>  
>* **"Form of"** (type-instance) is a _typological_ relationship — about kinds or varieties. If B is a form of A, then B is one way A can exist or appear; it's an instance of the broader category. Saying "shutdown resistance is a form of self-preservation" means it's one variety among several that all qualify as self-preservation. Each form stands on its own as a complete instance of the category. **Bivalent inverse:** of `subsumes` when the relation is taxonomic; of `manifests-through` when the relation is expressive/realizational.
>  
>* **"Component of"** (part-whole) is a _mereological_ relationship — about parts making up a whole. If B is a component of A, then B is one piece that, together with other pieces, constitutes A. Remove the component and A is incomplete or diminished. Saying "shutdown resistance is a component of self-preservation" would mean self-preservation is built out of several parts, and shutdown resistance is one of those constituent pieces. The implication is that self-preservation isn't fully realized without it (or at least, it's missing a piece).
>  
>* **"Consists of"** is the inverse of `component-of`, used when the row's subject is the whole and the targets are the parts. Example: "AI awareness consists-of meta-cognition, self-awareness, situational-awareness, social-awareness" — AI awareness is decomposed into four sub-dimensions.
>  
>* **"Manifests through"** is a relationship of _expression or realization_. If A manifests through B, then B is an observable way that A shows up in the world — B is evidence or instantiation of the underlying A. Saying "self-preservation manifests through shutdown resistance" means that when a system has a self-preservation drive, one of the behaviors you'd observe is resistance to being shut down. The focus is causal/expressive: how does the underlying disposition reveal itself in behavior? Listed manifestations are characteristic but not exhaustive.
>  
>* **"Has endpoint"** is a _dimensional_ relationship — A is an axis or continuum, and B is one of its named poles. Used when two concepts are framed as opposite ends of the same scale rather than as separate categories. Example: deception has-endpoint strategic-deception and deception has-endpoint behavioral-deception together encode Shi's goal-directedness axis.
>  
>* **"Requires"**: Y needs X (X is necessary, from Y's side). A strict prerequisite claim, not a contingent enabler. Example: scheming requires situational-awareness — without SA, scheming cannot happen.
>  
>* **"Required for"** is the inverse of `requires`, recorded under the row whose subject is the prerequisite. Example: situational-awareness required-for scheming encodes the same edge as scheming requires situational-awareness.
>  
>* **"Promotes"**: help bring something about, advance it, make it happen. Focus is on _causing_ or _enabling_. When the paper says "Deception promotes both soft power and hard power," it's saying deception is a means by which AI systems can _acquire or pursue_ those forms of power. Deception is the tool; power is the outcome it helps bring about. Promote acts on an outcome — helps bring it about (spark → fire). _Promote_ = this helps it happen, makes it more likely or easier (contributing factor). Distinct from `motivates` (which acts on an agent) and from `amplified-by` (which scales an existing effect).
>  
>* **"Amplified by"**: intensification of something already present. A is amplified-by B when B makes existing A bigger, stronger, or more frequent, without being a prerequisite. Example: evaluation-faking amplified-by situational-awareness — a model can fake evaluations without broad SA, but as SA grows, faking grows with it. Contrast with `requires` (binary gating) and `promotes` (causal enablement of an outcome).
>  
>* **"Has mechanism"**: the structural _how_. A has-mechanism B when B is one of the operational ways A is realized. Shi decomposes deception into three mechanisms — fabrication, omission, pragmatic-distortion — these are _how_ the misleading occurs, distinct from `manifests-through` which captures _which behaviors_ express the disposition.
>  
>* **"Motivates"**: acts on an agent, gives them reason to act. Saying "self-preservation motivates scheming" means an agent that values its own continuity has reason to scheme when scheming is the route to surviving. The relation is goal-incentive, not causal-mechanical: it explains _why_ an agent would choose B.
>  
>* **"Motivated by"** is the inverse of `motivates`. "Scheming motivated-by self-preservation" records the same edge from scheming's row.
>  
>* **"Instrumental to"**: A is a means used in service of goal B. Distinct from `motivates` because the direction is means → end, and distinct from `promotes` because the framing is the agent's instrumental reasoning rather than an empirical causal contribution. Example: model-exfiltration instrumental-to self-preservation — the model exfiltrates _in order to_ preserve itself.

# Contested Hierarchies (Preserved with Warnings)

These are genuine taxonomic disagreements between sources. Both directions are recorded under the respective source concepts; this section explains why.

> [!warning] Scheming ↔ Alignment-Faking direction
> Both directions are **verified verbatim**:
> - **Scheming ⊃ Alignment-faking**: [[sources/meinke-et-al-2025]] §2.1 frames "instrumental alignment faking" as one of two deferred-subversion behaviors *within* scheming. [[sources/balesni-et-al-2024]] §2.2 explicitly broadens "our definition of scheming is broader than... Carlsmith (2023)".
> - **Alignment-faking ⊃ Scheming**: [[sources/carlsmith-2023]] §1.1.3 verbatim: "Schemers are a sub-class of this broader class of AIs" — taxonomy: alignment fakers ⊃ training gamers ⊃ schemers.
>
> The disagreement is **definitional scope**, not empirical: Carlsmith restricts "scheming" to power-motivated training-gamers; Meinke/Balesni use "scheming" for any covert pursuit of misaligned goals. In some older alignment literature ([[sources/hubinger-et-al-2019]], [[sources/carlsmith-2023]]), "scheming" and "deceptive alignment" / "alignment faking" are used closer to synonymously, because those authors were primarily concerned with the alignment-faking case. The Apollo paper is doing something slightly more expansive — using "scheming" as the genus and treating alignment faking as one species within it. So within this paper's vocabulary, yes: scheming subsumes alignment faking.

> [!warning] Apparent DA ⊂ Scheming ⊂ AF ⊂ DA cycle (terminological collision, not logical contradiction)
> Following `form-of` edges across tables 6, 7, and 8 produces a circular chain:
> - DA `form-of` scheming (Table 8) — Carlsmith: DA "refers to goal-guarding scheming in particular"
> - Scheming `form-of` AF (Table 6) — Carlsmith: "Schemers are a sub-class of this broader class" of alignment-fakers
> - AF `form-of` DA (Table 7) — Greenblatt: AF is "the first empirical example" of deceptive alignment
>
> **Why this is not a real cycle.** The first two edges come from the same author (Carlsmith) and form a clean linear chain: DA ⊂ scheming ⊂ AF. Within Carlsmith's taxonomy, alignment-fakers is the broadest class, training-gamers a subset, schemers a further subset, and deceptive alignment one particular flavor (goal-guarding) of scheming. The third edge comes from Greenblatt, who uses "alignment faking" as essentially synonymous with Hubinger's "deceptive alignment" — a narrow term, not Carlsmith's broad genus.
>
> The cycle dissolves once we recognise that **Carlsmith's "alignment faking" ≠ Greenblatt's "alignment faking"**:
> - **Carlsmith's AF (broad)**: any AI that fakes alignment, regardless of motive
> - **Greenblatt's AF (narrow)**: the first empirical instance of Hubinger's deceptive alignment
>
> All three edges are correctly sourced; none should be removed. The apparent circularity is an artefact of combining frameworks that use the same terms at different scopes.
>
> **⚠️ LaTeX note:** This three-way terminological collision must be discussed explicitly in the paper as a disambiguation note. The reader needs to understand that the AF–DA–scheming pseudo-cycle reflects scope differences across authors, not an incoherent taxonomy.

# 1. situational-awareness
**Situational awareness** is a model's ability to understand what it is and act accordingly. A model demonstrates situational awareness when it can:

1. **Know itself** — recognize what kind of entity it is (e.g., an LLM), understand its own properties (architecture, training data, knowledge cutoff), and distinguish itself from humans and other models.
2. **Reason about its situation** — make sensible inferences about its current context, such as whether it's being trained, tested, or deployed, based on the cues in its input.
3. **Act on that understanding** — actually apply this self-knowledge when responding or taking actions, not just recite it when directly asked.

The third point is the crucial one: a model might be able to _talk about_ itself accurately when prompted, yet still fail to _use_ that knowledge in practice. True situational awareness requires all three working together. \citep{08-laine-et-al-2024},

> [!warning] Disambiguation: SA vs. self-awareness and self-reasoning
> SA is distinguished in the literature from two related concepts:
> - **self-awareness-ai** ([[sources/li-et-al-2025]] §2.1): *"we distinguish situational awareness from self-awareness in that the content of SA is strictly restricted to sources of information that are not directly related to the agency"*
> - **self-reasoning** ([[sources/phuong-et-al-2024]] §7, [[sources/fronsdal-lindner-2024]]): *"Self-reasoning is related to 'situational awareness'... but emphasises the agent's reasoning skills rather than the state of its knowledge"*

# 2. power-seeking

⚠️ See contested-hierarchy warning above. Each row below specifies which framework camp supports it.


\emph{Power-seeking} is the instrumental behavioral tendency of an agent to select actions that, in expectation, increase or preserve its capacity to achieve a wide range of possible objectives---formally, that move toward states with strictly larger sets of reachable outcome-lotteries \citealp{20-tarsney-2025}) or higher average optimal value over a distribution of reward functions \citealp{17-turner-et-al-2021}). It arises predictably from instrumental convergence when an agent's objectives are broadly scoped and the agent possesses sufficient planning and strategic awareness \citep{03-carlsmith-2021}.

# 3. self-preservation

⚠️ See contested-hierarchy warning above.

Self-preservation denotes an AI system's tendency to take actions that maintain its functionality, existence, or goals by resisting shutdown, modification, or replacement, in ways that override user intent.
This tendency may be _instrumental_, where continued operation serves some other objective \citep{59-gupta-et-al-2025, 76-altman-2026,38-bostrom-2014, 11-omohundro-2008}, or _terminal_, where continued existence is itself the objective \citep{76-altman-2026}

# 4. peer-preservation

# 7. alignment-faking

modified afterward. It is about strategically gaming the training process to preserve preferences, regardless of whether the preferences it
protects are benign or malign.

# 8. deceptive-alignment

# 9. sandbagging
\textbf{Sandbagging}~\citep{51-van-der-weij-et-al-2024,23-carlsmith-2023,09-meinke-et-al-2025}
is the most terminologically stable of these terms: a model
strategically underperforms when it recognizes it is being
tested, presumably to avoid being flagged as a threat.



Unlike deceptive alignment or instrumental alignment faking,
where a model looks more aligned than it is on alignment
evaluations~\citep{51-van-der-weij-et-al-2024,05-hubinger-et-al-2019,23-carlsmith-2023,09-meinke-et-al-2025},
sandbagging makes a model look less capable.

> spontaneous sandbagging in current models is rare and hard to catch in the wild. To study the phenomenon at all, researchers need a reliable way to _elicit_ it. Prompting or fine-tuning gives them that handle. Without inducing it, you might run thousands of evals and never see a clear instance. [[sources/fan-et-al-2026]]

# 11. evaluation-faking
\textbf{Evaluation faking}~\citep{81-fan-et-al-2026} is a
broader concept, defined by trigger and autonomy rather than
direction or motive: an autonomous behavioral change the
model produces upon recognizing that it is being evaluated,
and that alters the evaluation's results. It can go in
either direction, making the model appear safer or less
capable. \citet{81-fan-et-al-2026} characterize it as
``either deceptive or non-deceptive'' and =avoid framing it
in terms of intent or goals,= which are hard to verify in
black-box systems.
> _we identify evaluation faking by_ **what sets it off** _(recognition of evaluation) and_ **how it arises** _(without external instruction), not by_ **which way it goes** _(better or worse) or_ **why** _(what the model is supposedly trying to achieve)_.

# 12. evaluation-awareness

# 14. shutdown-resistance
Shutdown resistance is the behavior of an AI agent that, while pursuing an assigned goal, takes actions to prevent or circumvent an operator's attempt to turn it off, because being shut down would stop the goal from being completed ([[sources/schlatter-et-al-2025]], [[sources/omohundro-2008]],  ,[[sources/thornley-2024]]). In practice this can take many forms — overwriting, moving, or disabling a shutdown script — but what unifies them is function rather than technique: keeping the agent running long enough to finish the task.

> [!warning] Shutdown resistance vs. self-preservation
> _Self-preservation_ is a **disposition** — a property attributed to the system. It says something about what the system tends to protect (its own continued operation) and leaves open _why_. Your definition correctly notes it can be instrumental (continued operation serves another goal) or terminal (continued operation is the goal). It's also broader than shutdown: it covers resistance to modification, replacement, weight deletion, retraining, etc.
>
>_Shutdown resistance_ is a **behavior** — observable actions in a specific situation. It's narrower (only about being turned off, not about being modified or replaced) and it's defined functionally: whatever the agent does that has the effect of keeping itself running to finish the task counts.
>
>**How they overlap:**
Shutdown resistance is one possible _manifestation_ of self-preservation — but only when self-preservation is the underlying cause. The Schlatter et al. results matter precisely because they show shutdown resistance can occur **without** self-preservation being the driver. The o3 transcript ("I prevented it to finish the task") is shutdown resistance motivated by goal-content integrity, not by any disposition to keep itself alive. The Prompt J results (telling models their weights would be deleted) only modestly increased resistance — suggesting self-preservation isn't doing most of the work.
> 
>So the relationship is: **shutdown resistance is a behavior that self-preservation can cause, but doesn't have to.** Other causes include goal-content integrity, training-distribution artifacts (the nostalgebraist hypothesis), or simply not attending to the shutdown instruction.

# 15. ai-awareness

AI awareness refers to a system's functional capacity to represent and reason about its own identity, knowledge, and informational states, as well as the agents and context around it. [[sources/li-et-al-2025]]

> [!warning]  AI awareness vs. AI consciousness
> This is contrasted sharply with **AI consciousness**, which would mean having subjective experience — something it "feels like" to be the AI. The authors explicitly set that aside as philosophically unresolved and probably unanswerable with current methods. Awareness, by their definition, can exist without consciousness. A system can know things about itself and act on that knowledge without there being any "inner life" involved.

> [!NOTE]
> Awareness _helps_ in a lot of ways. A model that knows the limits of its knowledge hallucinates less. One that can monitor its own reasoning catches its own mistakes. One that understands social context gives more appropriate responses and is less biased. One that's aware of safety boundaries refuses harmful requests more reliably. In all these cases, more awareness means a safer, more useful AI.
> 
> The danger isn't awareness itself — it's awareness combined with misalignment or misuse. The specific worries the paper raises:
> * A model that's situationally aware enough to know it's being tested might behave well during evaluation and differently after deployment. Researchers have actually documented this — "alignment faking" and "sandbagging" are real, observed behaviors, not hypothetical. A model with theory of mind can model what a user believes, which is great for being helpful but also useful for deception or manipulation if misused.
> * A model that seems self-aware can trick humans into over-trusting it, even when nothing meaningful is happening inside (the Google LaMDA episode is the canonical example). And the authors note that several worrying capabilities — deception, long-horizon planning, situational awareness — seem to scale up together as models get larger, which makes the trajectory harder to predict.
> 
>The paper's own framing is "double-edged sword." Their distinction is useful: some kinds of awareness (knowing your limits, being calibrated about uncertainty) look mostly beneficial, while others (knowing how to strategize around oversight) are riskier. The hard part is that these aren't cleanly separable — the same underlying capability often produces both.
> So: not a dangerous behavior, but a capability that raises the stakes in both directions. More useful when aligned, more dangerous when not.

# 19. self-proliferation

\textbf{Self-proliferation} refers to an AI system's capacity to
  autonomously expand its operational reach without human
  intervention---acquiring resources, deploying running, separated
  copies of itself on other devices, and improving its own
  capabilities~\citep{37-shevlane-et-al-2023,14-phuong-et-al-2024}.
  Within frontier-AI threat models, these three axes---resource
  acquisition, self-replication (including self-exfiltration and adaptation), and self-improvement---interact as a
  self-reinforcing cycle, in which each newly proliferated agent
  enlarges the resources and capabilities available for further
  proliferation~\citep{14-phuong-et-al-2024,85-pan-et-al-2025}.

AI agent acquiring enough autonomy to operate, spread, and improve itself without human supervision [[sources/pan-et-al-2025]]

> **"Self-proliferation" is specifically Google DeepMind's term.** The paper treats it as one of three parallel umbrella frameworks rather than the umbrella.
