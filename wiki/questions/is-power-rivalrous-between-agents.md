# Is Power Rivalrous Between Agents?

## Why It Matters
If power-seeking by an AI agent necessarily implies disempowerment of other agents (including humans), then power-seeking poses a more direct existential risk. If power is not inherently rivalrous, then an AI agent could pursue power without catastrophic consequences for humanity. Understanding the conditions under which power is zero-sum vs. positive-sum is crucial for risk assessment.

## What Sources Say
- **[[sources/tarsney-2025]]** (§7): Identifies this as a key open question. Notes that "power as I have characterized it in this paper is not inherently rivalrous" — gives a pizza-ordering example where each person has absolute power because all share preferences and defer to each other. But argues that "when agents want different things and are not willing to defer to each other's preferences, power must be at least partly rivalrous: I have the power to get the outcome I want only if you lack the power to prevent it." Suggests that "an agent who seeks power will also, at least to some extent, seek to disempower other agents" under conditions of preference disagreement.
- **[[sources/carlsmith-2021]]**: Argues that an AI agent successfully pursuing power would result in humanity being "disempowered" and that this outcome would be intrinsically catastrophic — implicitly treating power as rivalrous in the context of misaligned superintelligence.

## Suggested Investigation
- Formal analysis of conditions under which power-seeking implies disempowerment in multi-agent sequential decision settings
- Modeling the relationship between preference alignment and power rivalrousness
- Investigating whether AI agents with partial alignment pursue power in rivalrous or non-rivalrous ways
