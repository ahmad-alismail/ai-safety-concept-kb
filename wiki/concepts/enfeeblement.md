# Enfeeblement

## Definitions
- **[[sources/hendrycks-et-al-2023]]**: "Even if we ensure that the many unemployed humans are provided for, we may find ourselves completely reliant on AIs. This would likely emerge not from a violent coup by AIs, but from a gradual slide into dependence. As society's challenges become ever more complex and fast-paced, and as AIs become ever more intelligent and quick-thinking, we may forfeit more and more functions to them out of convenience. In such a state, the only feasible solution to the complexities and challenges compounded by AIs may be to rely even more heavily on AIs. This gradual process could eventually lead to the delegation of nearly all intellectual, and eventually physical, labor to AIs. In such a world, people might have few incentives to gain knowledge and cultivate skills, potentially leading to a state of enfeeblement." (Section 3.2.2, citing Russell 2019) — classified as: **civilizational-level existential-risk pathway**; a gradual, non-violent loss of effective human control

## Classification
Level of analysis: **civilizational / sociotechnical failure mode** — not a property of any individual AI system but of the aggregate human–AI ecosystem. Enfeeblement is a route to existential catastrophe that does **not** require misalignment: even perfectly aligned, beneficial AIs can cause enfeeblement if they are sufficiently convenient and capable relative to humans.

Hendrycks et al. place enfeeblement within the AI-race risk category (Section 3), framing it as an outcome of corporate-race dynamics pushing automation forward. Notably, this is a **non-accident, non-malice, non-misalignment** pathway: enfeeblement arises from rational, informed choices by individuals and organizations to delegate to AIs for efficiency reasons. The WALL-E film is cited as the cultural reference point.

## Evidence Summary
- **Theoretical / civilizational**: The claim is essentially conceptual — there is no direct empirical evidence of AI-driven enfeeblement at civilizational scale, because such enfeeblement has not yet occurred. Hendrycks et al. argue for its plausibility by analogy to:
  - Historical automation reducing specific human skills (the paper does not cite specific empirical studies)
  - The "WALL-E scenario" as a cultural touchpoint
  - Russell's (2019) argument in Human Compatible that AIs may lead to "the enfeeblement of the human race"
- **Adjacent empirical work** (not cited by Hendrycks et al. but relevant): studies on over-reliance on automation in aviation, medicine, and navigation show localized skill atrophy; social-media dependence literature documents behavioral effects of AI-mediated platforms. These are suggestive but not direct evidence of civilizational-scale enfeeblement.

## Open Questions
- Is enfeeblement empirically distinguishable from ordinary automation-driven skill shifts, or is it a matter of degree rather than kind?
- What institutional mechanisms could preserve human capacity for self-government under widespread AI dependence?
- Does enfeeblement require fully-autonomous AIs, or can it arise from sufficiently helpful tool AIs?
- How does enfeeblement interact with [[concepts/concentration-of-power]] — do they compound, or is one a substitute for the other?
