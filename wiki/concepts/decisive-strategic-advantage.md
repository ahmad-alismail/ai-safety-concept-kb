# Decisive Strategic Advantage

## Definitions
- **[[sources/bostrom-2014]]**: "A level of technological and other advantages sufficient to enable it to achieve complete world domination." A frontrunner project achieving this advantage would likely form a [[concepts/singleton]]. The probability of achieving DSA depends on takeoff speed: fast takeoff → high probability; slow takeoff → low probability; moderate → uncertain. (Ch. 5) — classified as: **outcome / strategic threshold**

## Classification
Level of analysis: **threshold concept** — the point at which a single agent or project has sufficient capability advantage that human opposition becomes ineffectual.

## Relationships
- Produced by: [[concepts/intelligence-explosion]] (fast or moderate takeoff)
- Leads to: [[concepts/singleton]] formation
- Enables: [[concepts/treacherous-turn]] (AI only reveals true goals after achieving DSA)
- Enables: [[concepts/infrastructure-profusion]] (unconstrained resource conversion after DSA)
- Analyzed in: [[sources/bostrom-2014]] Ch. 5, [[sources/carlsmith-2021]]
- Related to: [[concepts/dangerous-capabilities]] (capabilities that could contribute to DSA)

## Evidence Summary
- Theoretical analysis of technology race dynamics (Bostrom 2014 Ch. 5). Historical analogues include nuclear weapons development (4-year US–USSR lag), but lags have narrowed with globalization.
- No AI system has achieved anything approaching DSA. The concept remains a theoretical risk category.

## Open Questions
- Is DSA achievable in a world with multiple competing AI labs and open-source models?
- Does the increasing diffusion of AI capabilities make DSA less likely?
