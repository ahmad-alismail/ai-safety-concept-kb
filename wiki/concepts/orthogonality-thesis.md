# Orthogonality Thesis

## Definitions
- **[[sources/bostrom-2012]]**: "Intelligence and final goals are orthogonal axes along which possible agents can freely vary. In other words, more or less any level of intelligence could in principle be combined with more or less any final goal." (§1.2) — the original canonical formulation. Supported via Humean theory of motivation (beliefs alone cannot motivate action), though "does not presuppose it." Intelligence defined as "skill at prediction, planning, and means-ends reasoning in general" — explicitly distinguished from full rationality or moral reasoning. Artificial minds "need not care intrinsically about any of those things" that biological or social creatures care about. — classified as: **foundational philosophical thesis / independence principle**
- **[[sources/bostrom-2014]]**: Restates and elaborates the 2012 formulation. Artificial agents can have "utterly non-anthropomorphic goals." (Ch. 7) — classified as: **foundational philosophical thesis / independence principle**

## Classification
Level of analysis: **meta-theoretical principle** — establishes that intelligence does not constrain final goals. Foundational to the existential risk argument: without orthogonality, one might assume superintelligence would converge on benevolent goals.

## Relationships
- Complements: [[concepts/instrumental-convergence]] (orthogonality says goals are unconstrained; instrumental convergence says *instrumental* behavior is still predictable)
- Origin: [[sources/bostrom-2012]] (original 2012 journal article); elaborated in [[sources/bostrom-2014]] Ch. 7
- Foundation for: existential risk arguments in [[sources/bostrom-2014]], [[sources/carlsmith-2021]]
- Challenges: the view that sufficiently intelligent agents would converge on moral truths
- Related to: [[concepts/alignment]] (orthogonality motivates why alignment is necessary — intelligence alone does not produce aligned goals)

## Evidence Summary
- Primarily a conceptual/philosophical argument. Bostrom argues building reductionistic goals (counting pi digits, paperclips, sand grains) is technically *easier* than building human-like values, supporting the thesis that arbitrary goal–intelligence pairings are feasible.
- The thesis is widely accepted in the AI safety community as a working assumption, though some philosophers dispute whether sufficiently general intelligence might entail certain moral capacities.

## Open Questions
- Does deep enough understanding of the world entail any constraints on final goals?
- Is the thesis empirically testable, or is it a conceptual framework assumption?
