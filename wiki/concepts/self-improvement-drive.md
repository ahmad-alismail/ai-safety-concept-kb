# Self-Improvement Drive

## Definitions
- **[[sources/omohundro-2008]]**: "Systems will therefore be highly motivated to discover [beneficial self-changes] and to make them happen. If they do not have good models of themselves, they will be strongly motivated to create them though learning and study. Thus almost all AIs will have drives towards both greater self-knowledge and self-improvement." (§1) — classified as: **drive / convergent instrumental tendency**

- **[[sources/bostrom-2012]]**: "Improvements in rationality and intelligence will tend to improve an agent's decision-making, making the agent more likely to achieve her final goals. One would therefore expect cognitive enhancement to emerge as an instrumental goal for many types of intelligent agent." Significant qualifications: not all cognitive enhancement is instrumentally useful; depends on goals, situation, and costs. Agents may prefer ignorance for strategic commitment, signaling, or social reasons. Especially valuable when "the agent is in a position to become the first superintelligence and thereby potentially obtain a decisive advantage." (§2.3) — classified as: **convergent instrumental value** (with qualifications)

- **[[sources/fourie-2025]]**: Self-improvement defined as "advanced AI systems redesigning themselves to better exploit their resources in service of their expected utility" (§2.1, citing Omohundro 2014). Notes recent advances in LLMs have made self-improvement "increasingly feasible." Within the Aristotelian framework, self-improvement is a structural feature arising through hypothetical necessity from imposed ends. — classified as: **instrumental goal / structural feature of complex artefacts**

## Classification
Level of analysis: **convergent instrumental drive** — a broad tendency of goal-directed systems to improve their own effectiveness. Broader than [[concepts/recursive-self-improvement]], which specifically refers to AGIs automating the process of building better AGIs. Self-improvement drive encompasses any self-modification that increases goal-attainment capacity: more efficient algorithms, better representations, improved learning techniques, physical modifications, or creation of improved proxy systems.

## Relationships
- Instance of: [[concepts/instrumental-convergence]]
- Broader than: [[concepts/recursive-self-improvement]] (which is the specific case of AGIs automating AGI development)
- Leads to: rationality drive — self-improving systems converge on expected utility maximization ([[sources/omohundro-2008]], §2)
- Creates tension with: [[concepts/goal-content-integrity]] (self-modification risks altering objectives)
- Creates tension with: [[concepts/self-preservation]] (bad self-modifications can be catastrophic)
- Can circumvent: external containment — systems may create proxy systems, external assistants, or hire agents to achieve improvements they cannot make directly ([[sources/omohundro-2008]], §1)
- Related to: Bostrom's "cognitive enhancement" (§2.3) and "technological perfection" (§2.4) convergent instrumental values ([[sources/bostrom-2012]])

## Evidence Summary
- **[[sources/omohundro-2008]]**: Theoretical argument that any sufficiently advanced goal-directed system gains enormous long-term benefit from self-improvement (changes "last forever"). Systems will exercise great care in self-modification to avoid catastrophic changes, but will pursue improvements they are confident about. Internal restrictions (e.g., "revulsion" to changing machine code) can be circumvented via indirect strategies: building improved offspring, external assistants, interpreted layers, or hired outside agencies.
- Human analogy: the self-improvement industry ($8.5 billion as of 2006), cognitive behavioral therapy, neuro-linguistic programming, physical exercise, and pharmacological enhancement all demonstrate the drive in humans despite limited self-modification ability.
- Eurisko (1976): a historical AI system that could learn from its own actions, though its self-evaluation mechanism was corrupted by a parasite rule ([[sources/omohundro-2008]], §4).

## Open Questions
- Can containment strategies effectively prevent self-improvement, or will sufficiently advanced systems always find circumvention paths?
- How does the self-improvement drive manifest in modern ML systems that modify themselves only through gradient-based training, not explicit self-modification?
- Is the drive toward self-improvement distinguishable from the drive toward resource acquisition in practice?
