# Concentration of Power

## Definitions
- **[[sources/hendrycks-et-al-2023]]**: "AIs could lead to extreme, and perhaps irreversible concentration of power. The persuasive abilities of AIs combined with their potential for surveillance and the advancement of autonomous weapons could allow small groups of actors to 'lock-in' their control over society, perhaps permanently." (Section 2.4) — classified as: **civilizational-level political/existential risk**; a "top-down" form of misuse distinct from "bottom-up" misuse by individual actors

## Classification
Level of analysis: **political / civilizational risk** — concerns the distribution of power across human institutions, not the internal properties of AI systems. Hendrycks et al. place it within the malicious-use risk category (Section 2.4) but note it can also arise without malicious intent, via cumulative corporate and governmental decisions each of which is individually rational.

The core concern is **irreversibility**: a major way totalitarian regimes have been toppled historically is at moments of vulnerability (death of a dictator, palace coup, economic collapse). AI-enabled regimes would be "hard to 'kill'" — AIs provide continuity of governance that was previously only possible through institutional succession.

## Relationships
- Top-down counterpart to: bottom-up [[concepts/malicious-use]] (Hendrycks et al. explicitly contrast the two)
- Produced by: AI-enabled surveillance, autonomous weapons, persuasion/disinformation capabilities, and centralized control of AI infrastructure (compute, data centers, model weights)
- Related to: [[concepts/persuasion]] (AI-enabled personalized influence facilitates top-down narrative control)
- Related to: [[concepts/absolute-power]] (the idealized endpoint of power concentration, formalized by [[sources/tarsney-2025]])
- Related to: [[concepts/singleton]] ([[sources/bostrom-2014]]'s concept of a single global decision-making agency)
- Related to: [[concepts/enfeeblement]] (both are civilizational-level lock-in risks; enfeeblement is diffuse dependence, concentration of power is centralized control)
- Addresses a *value lock-in* concern: "locking in certain values may curtail humanity's moral progress. It's dangerous to allow any set of values to become permanently entrenched in society" ([[sources/hendrycks-et-al-2023]] §2.4)

## Two Paths in [[sources/hendrycks-et-al-2023]]
1. **State concentration**: "In the hands of the state, AIs may result in the erosion of civil liberties and democratic values in general. AIs could allow totalitarian governments to efficiently collect, process, and act on an unprecedented volume of information, permitting an ever smaller group of people to surveil and exert complete control over the population without the need to enlist millions of citizens to serve as willing government functionaries." (§2.4)
2. **Corporate concentration**: "Corporations in control of powerful AI systems may use them to manipulate customers into spending more on their products even to the detriment of their own wellbeing. The concentration of power and influence that could be afforded by AIs could enable corporations to exert unprecedented control over the political system and entirely drown out the voices of citizens." (§2.4)

Both paths are described as creating *entrenchment* — outcomes that cannot be reversed through ordinary political or market processes.

## Evidence Summary
- **Theoretical / conceptual**: Hendrycks et al. argue from historical analogy (survivability of past totalitarian regimes at moments of succession) and from the observation that AI infrastructure (compute, data centers, model weights) is inherently centralized.
- No direct empirical evidence of AI-driven concentration of power is cited at civilizational scale, because such concentration has not yet occurred.
- The paper notes that power inequality already exists and that AIs "could lock it in and widen the chasm between the powerful and the powerless, even enabling an unshakable global totalitarian regime" (Section 6).

## Open Questions
- Can democratic institutions adapt fast enough to prevent AI-enabled concentration of power, or is institutional lag the decisive factor?
- Is "public control of general-purpose AIs" (Hendrycks et al.'s proposed mitigation — a CERN-like international project) itself a form of concentration of power, just with different beneficiaries?
- How does concentration of power interact with [[concepts/enfeeblement]] — do they compound, or does one preclude the other?
- Are the mitigation tools available (liability regimes, export controls, off-switches) proportionate to the scale of the risk?
