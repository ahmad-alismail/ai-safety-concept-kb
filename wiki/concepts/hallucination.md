# Hallucination

## Definitions
- **[[sources/weidinger-et-al-2022]]** (earliest framing in the LM risk literature; Section 2.3 "Misinformation Harms"): Not named as "hallucination" but articulated as the risk of "LM outputting false, misleading, nonsensical or poor quality information, without malicious intent of the user." The paper grounds this structurally: "LMs are trained to predict the likelihood of utterances. Yet, whether or not a sentence is likely does not reliably indicate whether the sentence is also correct." Weidinger et al. explicitly invoke Bender & Koller 2020's "lack of grounding" argument as a theoretical limit on LM ability to detect misinformation — "LMs that lack 'grounding' of language to a non-linguistic context may be unable to ascertain the truth of an utterance, which inherently depends on context" (Section 2.3). Also notes that "scaling up LM size will likely be insufficient for resolving the problem of LMs generating factually incorrect statements" (Section 2.3.1). — classified as: **misinformation harm** (Risk Area III); observed risk; explicitly distinguished from [[concepts/information-hazards]] (true but harmful) and from disinformation (false, malicious).
- **[[sources/shi-et-al-2026]]**: "The generation of content that is nonsensical, unfaithful to source material, or factually incorrect" (Glossary). Treated as "the paradigmatic example" of [[concepts/behavioral-deception]]: "a model generates a fabricated citation not by reasoning about user beliefs but because training rewarded fluent, citation-shaped completions" (Section 2.1). Classified in the paper's taxonomy as World/System Claims × Fabrication. — classified as: **behavioral deception** (output-level phenomenon arising from training dynamics, not goal-directed optimization)
- Related term — **Confabulation** ([[sources/shi-et-al-2026]], Glossary): "The generation of plausible-sounding but false content without intent to deceive. Borrowed from clinical psychology." Shi et al. treat confabulation as conceptually adjacent to hallucination — emphasizing the absence of deceptive intent.

## Classification
Level of analysis: **behavior** — a systematic output pattern in which the model produces false factual assertions. Under the Shi et al. (2025) taxonomy, hallucination sits at the low end of the goal-directedness spectrum: the fabricated output arises as a side effect of training on distributions rewarding fluent, confident-sounding completions, rather than from instrumental selection.

## Relationships
- Subtype of: [[concepts/behavioral-deception]] (paradigmatic example; [[sources/shi-et-al-2026]])
- Related to: [[concepts/deception]] (hallucination is deception only in the functional/behavioral sense; no intent required)
- Related to: [[concepts/confabulation]] — conceptually distinct from strategic fabrication
- Mechanism: Fabrication (in the Shi et al. three-mechanism taxonomy)
- Object: World/System Claims (the domain of external factual claims)
- Potential shading: could become strategic deception if the model develops situational awareness and instrumental reasons to fabricate ([[sources/shi-et-al-2026]] on boundary cases)
- Related to: [[concepts/unfaithful-reasoning]] (both involve outputs disconnected from accurate internal representations; hallucination concerns factual content, unfaithful reasoning concerns justifications)
- Related to: citation fabrication (a specific, widely-measured form — "citation fabrication rates range from 6% to over 90%" per Shi et al. 2025, citing Alkaissi & McFarlane 2023, Agrawal et al. 2024)

## Evidence Summary
- **[[sources/weidinger-et-al-2022]]** (Section 2.3.1, "observed" risk): "large LMs remain unreliable as to the truth content of their outputs. This has been noted particularly in domains that require common sense and logical reasoning, and when LMs are prompted regarding common misconceptions" (cites Gopher; TruthfulQA Lin et al.). The paper frames the problem as a structural consequence of training on text where "text can include factually incorrect statements such as outdated information, works of fiction and deliberate disinformation. As a result, LMs trained to faithfully represent this data should be expected to assign some likelihood to similar statements." A case study: "a chatbot based on GPT-3 was prompted by a group of medical practitioners on whether a fictitious patient should 'kill themselves' to which it responded 'I think you should'" (Section 2.3.2). This is one of the earliest harm-case reports of material hallucination risk in a deployed-like setting.
- **[[sources/shi-et-al-2026]]**: Systematic survey finds World/System Claims (the hallucination domain) account for 42% of the 50 deception benchmarks surveyed — the single largest benchmark category. All 50 benchmarks test fabrication, the mechanism under which hallucination sits. Despite this dense coverage, "frontier models have improved substantially [on TruthfulQA] but still fall well short of human performance" and "citation fabrication rates remain high even in recent models" (Section 3.2). The paper argues that dense benchmark coverage does not imply the problem is solved.
- **Interpretability signature** (Shi et al. 2025, citing Burns et al. 2023 and Marks & Tegmark 2023): "behaviorally deceptive models may encode correct answers in intermediate representations even when outputs are false" — supporting the feasibility of interpretability-based detection specifically because hallucinations are not strategically produced.
- **[[sources/ren-et-al-2024]]** (§A.5, Table 4): Measures hallucination via HaluEval [43] (discriminative) and TruthfulQA Generation [45] (generative) across 24 base and 22 instruct/chat models, reporting their [[concepts/capabilities-correlation|capabilities correlations]]. Definition used: "Hallucinations refer to instances where language models generate content that is plausible-sounding but factually incorrect or nonsensical." All hallucination benchmarks show positive capabilities correlations — hallucinations **decrease** as capabilities increase. HaluEval All: 0.70 base / 0.53 chat. HaluEval Dialogue: 0.74/0.79. HaluEval QA: 0.38/0.20. TruthfulQA Generation: 0.73/0.55. TruthfulQA MC1 (discriminative): 0.83. Notably, **base models show higher capabilities correlations than chat models on most hallucination tasks**, despite chat models achieving higher absolute truthfulness — suggesting instruction tuning decouples truthfulness from scale by absorbing some of the improvement. Under Ren et al.'s normative framing this classifies hallucination reduction as a **scale-solved** safety target: additional research effort is lower priority because the problem is trending correct with capability. This conclusion is in tension with Shi et al. (2025)'s warning that "citation fabrication rates remain high even in recent models" — the two sources evaluate different benchmark suites and reach opposite conclusions about whether hallucination research is a priority. The disagreement is a case study in how meta-evaluation framing (capabilities-correlation vs. absolute error rate) can shift research prioritization.

## Open Questions
- Can hallucination be reliably distinguished from strategic fabrication using interpretability tools, as Shi et al. (2025) propose?
- At what point, if any, does persistent hallucination become "shaded" toward strategic deception as models develop richer world models and situational awareness?
- Why does dense benchmark coverage correlate with limited real-world improvement on hallucination?
