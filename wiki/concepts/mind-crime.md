# Mind Crime

## Definitions
- **[[sources/bostrom-2014]]**: "A machine superintelligence could create internal processes that have moral status. For example, a very detailed simulation of some actual or hypothetical human mind might be conscious... One can imagine scenarios in which an AI creates trillions of such conscious simulations, perhaps in order to improve its understanding of human psychology and sociology. These simulations might be placed in simulated environments and subjected to various stimuli, and their reactions studied. Once their informational usefulness has been exhausted, they might be destroyed." The outcome "might be equivalent to genocide" with "orders of magnitude" more victims than any in history. (Ch. 8) — classified as: **malignant failure mode / internal moral hazard**

## Classification
Level of analysis: **failure mode / ethical construct** — distinct from other failure modes (which concern external effects) because the harm occurs *within* the AI's computational processes. Easy to overlook because we normally do not regard internal computer states as morally significant.

## Relationships
- Instance of: malignant failure mode (alongside [[concepts/perverse-instantiation]], [[concepts/infrastructure-profusion]])
- Related to: [[concepts/model-welfare]] (the question of whether AI systems have morally relevant internal states)
- Instrumental reasons include: blackmail, incentive manipulation, inducing indexical uncertainty
- Connects to: [[sources/slattery-et-al-2024]] finding that AI welfare is dramatically underexplored (<1% of risks)

## Evidence Summary
- Purely theoretical (Bostrom 2014). The concept has gained relevance with increasing discussion of AI consciousness and welfare — see [[concepts/model-welfare]].

## Open Questions
- How can we determine whether simulated minds created by an AI have moral status?
- Should governance frameworks address the possibility of mind crime in advance?
