# Absolute Power

## Definitions
- **[[sources/tarsney-2025]]**: "the ability to bring about any outcome with probability 1." Absolute power has maximal expected utility for any expected utility maximizer with non-uniform preferences. Ranked strictly above any other situation by power relations ≻p1 and ≻p3. "Nearly absolute power" (ability to bring about nearly any outcome with near-certainty) is nearly as attractive as fully absolute power due to continuity. — classified as: **formal concept / idealized endpoint of power-seeking**

## Classification
Level of analysis: **formal decision-theoretic concept** — an idealization that is "very unlikely to be realized in practice" but serves as an analytical tool for understanding when instrumental convergence predictions are most informative.

## Relationships
- Endpoint of: [[concepts/power-seeking]]
- Restores predictiveness of: [[concepts/instrumental-convergence]] (when near-absolute power is attainable, Proposition 5's incompleteness limitation matters less)
- Related to: [[concepts/resource-acquisition]] (resources contribute to power, but absolute power is a stronger condition)
- Introduced by: [[sources/tarsney-2025]] (§6)

## Evidence Summary
- Tarsney (2025) argues that instrumental convergence results are "more likely to be predictive of the behavior of future artificial agents insofar as we expect those agents to have opportunities to acquire absolute or near-absolute power." If future AI agents have superhuman capabilities giving them a shot at near-absolute power, then the incompleteness of partial power rankings does not undermine the convergence prediction.
- Conversely, "if you expect future AI agents to be embedded in a complex multipolar world... where even the most powerful individuals cannot aspire to anything like absolute power, and the pursuit of power typically carries great risks and forecloses certain desirable options... then the fact that power is a convergent instrumental goal may end up telling us relatively little."

## Open Questions
- How close to "absolute power" could a superintelligent AI realistically get in a complex multipolar world?
- Does the availability of near-absolute power change the cost-benefit calculus of power-seeking for agents with diverse utility functions?
