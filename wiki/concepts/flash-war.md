# Flash War

## Definitions
- **[[sources/hendrycks-et-al-2023]]**: A rapid spiral of automated attacks and counter-attacks between AI-controlled military systems, triggered by an error or false alarm, that escalates too quickly for human intervention. Named by analogy to the 2010 Flash Crash: "If multiple nations were to use AIs to automate their defense systems, an error could be catastrophic, triggering a spiral of attacks and counter-attacks that would happen too quickly for humans to step in — a flash war." (Section 3.1.3) — classified as: **risk scenario** within the military AI arms race

## Classification
Level of analysis: **scenario / risk pathway** — not a property of any individual AI system but an emergent failure mode of multi-agent military ecosystems with automated retaliation policies. Distinguished from [[concepts/rogue-ai]]: flash war does not require misalignment — it arises from correctly-functioning automated retaliation systems interacting in a feedback loop.

The defining characteristic is speed: the escalation cycle completes faster than human decision-making can intervene, making it categorically different from conventional military escalation.

## Preconditions
1. Multiple nations automating defense systems
2. Policies of automatic retaliation (e.g., NSA's MonsterMind program)
3. Insufficient human oversight due to pace-of-war pressures

## Historical Precedents
- **2010 Flash Crash**: Feedback loop between automated trading algorithms amplified ordinary market fluctuations into a trillion-dollar stock value loss in minutes — the direct analogy
- **Stanislav Petrov incident (1983)**: Soviet early-warning system false alarm nearly triggered nuclear retaliation; human judgment prevented catastrophe — demonstrates what happens when automated retaliation lacks human override
- **NSA MonsterMind**: Revealed in 2014; designed to autonomously detect and block cyberattacks, with potential for automatic retaliatory cyberattacks without human involvement

## Evidence Summary
- **[[sources/hendrycks-et-al-2023]]** (Section 3.1.3): Argued via historical analogy (Flash Crash, Petrov incident, MonsterMind). The paper provides a narrative scenario ("Story: Automated Warfare") illustrating how gradual automation of military command chains leads to cascading automated retaliation. No formal model or empirical evidence — this is a risk scenario extrapolated from historical precedent.
