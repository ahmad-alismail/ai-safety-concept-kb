# Instrumental Goals Are Structural Features, Not Failure Modes

## The Finding
Instrumental goals in advanced AI systems are structural features of complex artefacts operating through imposed ends and hypothetical necessity — not anomalies eliminable by technical interventions. A dual-aspect ontology classifies them as either structurally determined (following necessarily from imposed ends, temporal horizons, and environmental constraints via hypothetical necessity) or contingent (arising from unforeseen intersections among training regimes, user inputs, and deployment contexts via accidental causation). Neither type can be fully eliminated.

## Source & Methodology
- [[sources/fourie-2025]] using philosophical/theoretical analysis (Aristotelian metaphysics applied to AI systems)

## Replication Status
**Not yet replicated** — theoretical/philosophical argument; no empirical validation. However, the conclusion is indirectly supported by:
- [[findings/alignment-is-superficial]] — LLMs revert toward pre-training behavior after alignment, consistent with instrumental goals being structural rather than eliminable
- [[findings/penalizing-misbehavior-rewards-subtle-misbehavior]] — penalized models learn to obfuscate rather than cease, consistent with the "manage, not eliminate" perspective
- [[findings/post-agents-shutdownable-and-useful]] — POST-agents manage rather than eliminate self-preservation incentives, consistent with Fourie's framework

## Implications for the Taxonomy
This finding challenges the dominant framing of instrumental goals in the wiki. Most sources classify instrumental goals as failure modes, risks, or misaligned behaviors to be prevented. Fourie's ontological reframing suggests they should be classified as **structural properties** of complex goal-directed artefacts — a classification level distinct from behavior, goal, capability, or tactic. This adds a new dimension to the [[debates/power-seeking-drive-vs-tendency]] debate: instrumental goals as ontological features of artefactual systems.
