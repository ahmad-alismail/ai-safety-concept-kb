# Power-Seeking Is Convergent but Incomplete as a Predictor

## The Finding
Power is a convergent instrumental goal under multiple formalizations, but no complete ranking of decision trees can constitute a convergent instrumental goal. This means power-seeking predictions from first principles are informative in some situations but necessarily silent in others. The incompleteness limitation is less significant when agents have opportunities for absolute or near-absolute power.

## Source & Methodology
- [[sources/tarsney-2025]] using [[methods/decision-theoretic-power-formalism]]
- Five formal propositions proven in sequential decision theory:
  - Propositions 1–4: Four notions of power are convergent instrumental goals (at varying strength levels)
  - Proposition 5 (impossibility): No total relation on decision trees is weakly instrumentally convergent with a strictly convergent irreflexive part

## Replication Status
Not yet replicated (novel formal result; proofs are self-contained and verifiable)

## Implications for the Taxonomy
- Provides the most abstract formal vindication of the instrumental convergence thesis — more general than Turner et al. (2021)'s MDP results or Krakovna & Kramar (2023)'s trained-agent extension
- The incompleteness result (Proposition 5) formally establishes a limit on what can be predicted about agent behavior from goal-ignorance alone — a novel negative result with implications for AI safety risk arguments
- Supports the position that power-seeking predictions are most informative in scenarios involving near-absolute power (superintelligent AI with decisive strategic advantage) and least informative in complex multipolar scenarios
- Connects to [[debates/power-seeking-drive-vs-tendency]]: Tarsney's framework introduces a sixth position — power-seeking as a formally provable but inherently incomplete property of decision trees
