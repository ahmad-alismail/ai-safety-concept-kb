# The hot mess theory of AI misalignment: More intelligent agents behave less coherently

| Field     | Value                                                                  |
| --------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Authors   | [[entities/jascha-sohl-dickstein\|Jascha Sohl-Dickstein]]              |
| Year      | 2023                                                                   |
| Venue/URL | Blog post — https://sohl-dickstein.github.io/2023/03/09/coherence.html |

## Key Claims
- The standard AGI misalignment narrative implicitly assumes that superintelligent AI will be *supercoherent* — monomaniacally pursuing a well-defined goal (§ Superintelligence vs. supercoherence)
- **The hot mess theory of intelligence**: "The more intelligent an agent is, the less coherent its behavior tends to be. Colloquially: getting smarter makes you a hotter mess." (§ Superintelligence vs. supercoherence)
- In a pilot experiment, human subjects judged more intelligent entities (organisms, humans, organizations, ML models) as less coherent — anticorrelation across all four categories (§ Getting smarter makes you a hotter mess)
- At the same estimated intelligence, ML models are judged far less coherent than living creatures; evolution may select for coherence in biological organisms (§ Each category has its own relationship)
- Human organizations may achieve higher coherence than individuals through structured internal rules (§ Each category has its own relationship)
- Intelligence judgments are consistent across subjects, but coherence judgments vary wildly — different subjects may interpret coherence differently (§ Human judgments of intelligence are consistent...)
- Misalignment from incoherence (no consistent goal) is more likely in practice than misalignment from supercoherence (wrong goal pursued relentlessly); incoherent AI will be "obvious at every stage of development" (§ Closing thoughts, § AI alignment is still important)
- The common misaligned AGI story assumes bias dominates (reliably pursuing wrong goal), while the hot mess hypothesis predicts variance dominates (random things not in pursuit of any consistent goal) (footnote 7)
- Coherence is not automatic for ML models: models are used in contexts different from training where behavior may not be interpretable as optimizing any meaningful objective; RL training "resembles integrating a non-conservative dynamical system more than it resembles optimizing any fixed objective" (footnote 6)
- Complex systems are harder to constrain to single objectives: "Requiring that a system act only in pursuit of a well-defined goal, or only to maximize a utility function, is an extremely strong constraint on its behavior. This constraint should become harder to satisfy as the system becomes more intelligent, and thus more complex." (§ Superintelligence vs. supercoherence)
- If intelligence and coherence start increasing together (rather than being anticorrelated), this would be a warning sign for the classical misalignment scenario (§ Ways in which this conclusion could be misleading)
- More intelligent entities may be simultaneously less coherent but more *effective* at achieving their objectives — raw capabilities may increase faster than coherence decreases (§ Ways in which this conclusion could be misleading)
- AI risk theories should be experimentally grounded, not merely argued: "Scientific fields which are not grounded in experiments or formal validation make silently incorrect conclusions" — illustrated with computational neuroscience analogy (§ Experimentally ground AI risk assessment)

## Methodology
- **Type**: Human-subject pilot experiment with subjective rank ordering
- **Subjects**: 14 PhD-level researchers (neuroscience/ML backgrounds)
- **Entities**: 60 entities across 4 categories (ML models, non-human organisms, well-known humans, human institutions) — generated by 4 separate subjects
- **Protocol**: 5 subjects sorted all 60 entities by intelligence; 6 subjects sorted by coherence; each subject judged only ONE attribute; hypothesis not shared with subjects; randomized initial ordering
- **Aggregation**: Averaged rank orders with standard error bars
- **Controls**: Entity lists generated by subjects (not experimenter); each subject rated only one attribute to prevent relationship consideration; randomized ordering
- **Acknowledged limitations**: "should be interpreted as a pilot experiment, and the results should be taken as suggestive rather than definitive"; small sample size; homogeneous subject backgrounds; subjective nature of intelligence/coherence ratings; coherence may be interpreted differently by different subjects
- **Suggested improvements**: 2AFC with Elo scoring; broader subject pool; broader entity set; objective proxies for intelligence (parameter count, encephalization quotient, IQ) and coherence (robustness to perturbation, resource allocation efficiency); preregistration
- **Open data**: Analysis Colab and anonymized experimental data publicly available

## Limitations
- Pilot study with only 14 subjects — results are suggestive, not definitive
- Subjective ratings of intelligence and coherence may not map to the constructs used in AGI risk arguments
- Coherence ratings inconsistent across subjects — the construct may be poorly defined or interpreted differently
- The observed anticorrelation might break at higher intelligence levels — sufficiently intelligent entities may introspect and increase their own coherence
- Cross-category comparisons (humans vs. organizations vs. ML models) are difficult to interpret
- Does not address whether effective capability (raw capability × coherence fraction) increases or decreases with intelligence — this is an empirically resolvable ambiguity
- The author explicitly does NOT argue against alignment work — argues for different types of alignment priorities
