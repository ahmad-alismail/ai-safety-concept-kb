## Key Claims
- **The Orthogonality Thesis**: "Intelligence and final goals are orthogonal axes along which possible agents can freely vary. In other words, more or less any level of intelligence could in principle be combined with more or less any final goal." (§1.2) — the canonical formulation
- **The Instrumental Convergence Thesis**: "Several instrumental values can be identified which are convergent in the sense that their attainment would increase the chances of the agent's goal being realized for a wide range of final goals and a wide range of situations, implying that these instrumental values are likely to be pursued by many intelligent agents." (§2) — the canonical formulation
- Five convergent instrumental values identified: [[concepts/self-preservation]] (§2.1), [[concepts/goal-content-integrity]] (§2.2), cognitive enhancement (§2.3; see [[concepts/self-improvement-drive]]), technological perfection (§2.4), [[concepts/resource-acquisition]] (§2.5)
- Goal-content integrity is "in a sense even more fundamental than survival as a convergent instrumental motivation" for software agents (§2.2)
- Resource acquisition is potentially unlimited: declining marginal cost of extraterrestrial resources via von Neumann probes makes expansion worthwhile even with steeply declining returns (§2.5)
- A superintelligence with benign-sounding goals (e.g., calculating pi) "would have a convergent instrumental reason, in many situations, to acquire an unlimited amount of physical resources and, if possible, to eliminate potential threats to itself and its goal system" (Conclusions)
- Relying on instrumental values as safety guarantors is dangerous — cooperative behavior may be abandoned when instrumental calculations change (Conclusions)
- Three approaches to predicting superintelligent behavior: (1) design competence, (2) inheritance from human template, (3) convergent instrumental reasons (§1.3)
- Conditions under which agents may intentionally change final goals: social signaling, social preferences, preferences about own goal content, storage costs (§2.2)

## Methodology
Pure philosophical/conceptual analysis. Develops two theses through logical reasoning, thought experiments (paperclip maximizer, sand-grain counter, Future-Tuesday-Indifference), and engagement with philosophical literature (Hume on motivation, Parfit on rationality). Systematic case analysis of five convergent instrumental values with qualifications and countervailing factors for each.

## Relationship to Other Sources
- **[[sources/omohundro-2008]]**: Agrees on the underlying idea of convergent instrumental behavior but explicitly rejects the "basic AI drives" terminology: "The term 'AI drive' has the advantage of being short and evocative, but it has the disadvantage of suggesting that the instrumental goals to which it refers influence the AI's decision-making in the same way as psychological drives influence human decision-making, i.e. via a kind of phenomenological tug on our ego which our willpower may occasionally succeed in resisting. That connotation is unhelpful." Also notes "more substantial" differences in treatment, though the paper does not elaborate on these.
- **[[sources/bostrom-2014]]**: This 2012 paper is the original journal article from which Chapter 7 of *Superintelligence* draws. The book expands and elaborates the arguments presented here. The verbatim definitions of both theses are nearly identical in the two sources.
- **[[sources/thornley-2024]]**: Cites this paper (as "Bostrom 2012") to ground self-preservation as the core motivation for the [[concepts/shutdown-problem]].
- Draws support from Hume's theory of motivation (beliefs alone cannot motivate action) and engages with Parfit's concept of irrational preferences (Future-Tuesday-Indifference).

## Limitations
- Pure philosophical argument with no empirical methodology — the theses are conceptual frameworks, not testable predictions
- The paper focuses on "intelligence" defined as instrumental rationality, deliberately sidestepping whether sufficiently general intelligence might entail moral capacities
- The convergent instrumental values are derived for a hypothetical superintelligent agent — the argument's force weakens for sub-superintelligent or loosely agentic systems
- The qualifications on each convergent instrumental value (especially cognitive enhancement and technological perfection) are significant but receive less emphasis than the headline claims
