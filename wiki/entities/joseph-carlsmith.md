# Joseph Carlsmith

## Profile
Researcher at Open Philanthropy focused on existential risk from artificial intelligence. Author of influential risk assessment reports analyzing the probability of existential catastrophe from misaligned AI.

## Key Contributions
- [[sources/carlsmith-2021]] — "Is Power-Seeking AI an Existential Risk?" (2021) — a detailed probabilistic risk assessment arguing for ~5% (later >10%) chance of existential catastrophe from misaligned power-seeking AI by 2070.
- [[sources/carlsmith-2023]] — "Scheming AIs: Will AIs fake alignment during training in order to get power?" (2023) — the most detailed theoretical treatment of scheming/deceptive alignment; introduces four-way model class taxonomy; estimates ~25% conditional probability of scheming.

## Conceptual Framework
Carlsmith's framework centers on **APS systems** (Advanced, Planning, Strategically aware) as the relevant system type for existential risk, deliberately setting a lower capability bar than "superintelligence" or "AGI." He frames power-seeking as an instrumental behavioral tendency arising from misalignment via instrumental convergence, avoiding utility maximization and "AI drives" framings. His risk assessment uses a six-premise conditional probability argument.

In his 2023 scheming report, Carlsmith introduces a detailed **model-class taxonomy** organized by motivational structure: alignment fakers → training-gamers (terminal vs. instrumental) → schemers (goal-guarding variant). He uses "scheming" instead of "deceptive alignment" to avoid conflation between broad alignment faking and specific goal-guarding behavior. He emphasizes the distinction between the "incentivized episode" and the "intuitive episode" as critical for assessing beyond-episode goals.

Key methodological choices:
- Avoids reliance on models of "utility function maximization"
- Does not assume fast/discontinuous take-off or recursive self-improvement
- Focuses on power-seeking specifically (not misalignment more broadly) as route to existential catastrophe
- Treats multipolar scenarios as equally concerning as unipolar ones
- Uses "scheming" terminology to disentangle different forms of AI deception
- Balances "counting argument" for scheming against "speed cost" arguments against it
